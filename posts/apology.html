In the opening pages, Socrates suggests that he is not a skilled or persuasive political speaker (17b-17d).
Does this tell us something about Socrates? What he values, where he has devoted his time?
Why might it seem important to the average Athenian to be skilled in public persuasion?
By spending his time elsewhere, and not in the political arena, what value has he chosen instead?
Why might it be difficult to pursue the goals associated with a skilled political life and also the goals Socrates has chosen at the same time? Are there reasons to think these goals might be incompatible?
What are the two different accusations levelled at Socrates? How does this "structure" the Dialogue" (18b-e, p. 23)

Tip: When reading a Platonic dialogue, always look for hints or clues to the internal structure of the work. This structure can help identify distinct steps in the argument or different themes being addressed.

If the first set of accusations are not true, what did Socrates do that made people so angry? (20b and following).
Consider the important statement: "What has caused my reputation a certain kind of wisdom?" What do you think Socrates means by this? What kind of "wisdom" is this?
Why does this wisdom end up making everyone so angry? Why does his constant examination of self and others irritate everyone so much?
Does this hint at why it might be difficult to pursue both power and truth or justice? How so?
