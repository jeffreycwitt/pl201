A Question for Thales

Thales wanted to find a single cause or explanation for the universe. In pursuit of such an explanation he proposed that the entire universe is fundamentally made up of one element: water

But if everything is water, how is Thales to account for our "lived experience" of variety and difference. Maybe everything is water. But why doesn't it all look like water?

If you think about it, this is still a question today that needs explaining.

I can point to an ice cube and you can tell me it is water. But then I can point to a glass of water and you will tell me it is also water. Again, I can point to a cloud in the sky and again you will tell me it is also water.

How can three different things, with such diverse properties, appearances, and behaviors really be the "same" thing?

Anaximenes on Condensation and Rarefaction

Anaximenes took the above question seriously.

If we're going to be able to explain the world in terms of a single cause (where this cause is some singular "stuff"), then the burden is on us to explain how this stuff can appear so "qualitatively different".
