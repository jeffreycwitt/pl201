Class 2 Reading Questions

Pieper, Leisure: The Basis of Culture, pp. 53-74

What objection does Pieper assert at the outset of chapter four?

show suggestion

How does Pieper define the proletariat?
Why is this definition not limited to manual work alone (think back to chapter 2)?
What are the three conditions that lead to "proletarization"?
How do each of these conditions bind people to "servile" work?
How do these conditions line up with the three things needed for "deproletarization"?
Why is property needed for "de-proletarization"?
Why are political and economic changes not enough (i.e. not a "sufficient condition") for "de-proletarization"?
How does this 'inadequacy' function as a transition between chapters four and five?
Consider the first sentence of chapter five in light the main points of chapter four. What's the connection?
Why do you think Pieper begin to make appeals to religion in chapter five?
What is the "heart of leisure" according to Pieper?
What connections do you seen between Pieper's discussion of leisure as "festival" and "worship" in chapter 5 and his description of leisure activity in chapter 2?
Consider in particular his discussion of intellectual contemplation as a kind of "receiving" rather than "taking". How might the ideas of contemplative "receiving" and "festival" be connected?
In other words: why does he think a certain kind of religious sensibility is required in order for people to truly engage in leisure activity?
