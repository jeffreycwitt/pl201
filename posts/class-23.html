Questions Aristotle, Metaphysics, Book I, pp. 221-240

Consider Aristotle's definition of wisdom in Book I, c. 1 closely.
Why does he not think that wisdom can be acquired through the senses?
Why is the master craftsman wiser than the manual craftsman?
According to Book I, c. 2, what kinds of causes does the wise person seek?
What are the four kinds of causes introduced in chapter 3?
Which causes did we focus on in our reading from the Physics? Which have we not yet looked at closely?
According to Aristotle, which types of causes did the early philosophers focus on?
Why does this narrow focus lead directly to the conclusion that nothing changes? (See 983b10)
According to Aristotle, what eventually pushes these philosophers to look beyond the purely material cause (984a17-27)?
In what way, did they discover the "efficient cause"?
What is Aristotle's view toward Plato?
Why does the combination of "Heraclitean beliefs" and concern for eternal definitions lead Plato to posit a separate world of Forms?
Why is Aristotle critical of the notion that Forms exist in a separate world? (See both chapter 6 and 9.)
Why, according to Aristotle, was the efficient cause eventually seen as insufficient? In what way do you see this insufficiency leading to something similar to Socrates' concern for the best arrangement and the discovery of the Forms or formal causality (see esp. 984b10)?

Questions for Metaphysics, Book XII, pp. 332–344

What is the central puzzle introduced in Book XII, c. 6?
Why does Aristotle say that there "must be a principle of the sort whose essence is actuality" (1071b19)?
What problem would occur if everything in the world only had the potentiality for motion or change, but there was no actuality that could come along and initiate that change?
How is Plato said to address this issue?
In the first paragraph of chapter 7, what are the two kinds of movers that Aristotle introduces to solve the problem introduced in chapter 6?
How are these movers similar?
How are these movers different?
Consider how this analysis of eternal motion in terms of an unmoved mover and everlasting circular movement is mapped on to Aristotle's vision of the stars and the planets in chapter 8.
Finally, in chapter 9, how does Aristotle describe the divine life of the unmoved mover?
Why does he argue that, if the unmoved mover is the best substance, it will be engaged in an ever-lasting contemplation of itself (1074b34)?
According to the second paragraph of chapter 7, how does the substance that "initiates motion without being moved" initiate motion?
In what way is this comparable to an object of love initiating movement in the lover? How so?
Can you think of an example of this kind of initiation of movement?
