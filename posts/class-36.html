Class 36 Guided Reading Questions

Plato’s Gorgias, 492d-506c, pp. 79-103

show participation instructions

How does Socrates turn the discussion in a new direction at the opening of this section?
Why does Callicles believe self-discipline to be “folly”?
Accordingly, to Callicles, why do we typically praise self-control/temperance and justice?
Does he take a different view towards courage?
In short, what is the happy life according to Callicles (bttm. of p. 79)?
And why aren’t most people capable of living it? What do they lack?
In response, Socrates invokes a couple of examples involving leaky jars. What is his point here? What do these examples illustrate?
On page 81, what is the alternative vision of the happy life that Socrates wants to present to Callicles?
Socrates proposes another example of Callicles’ description of the happy life: the life of perpetual scratching and itching (pp. 82-83).
Why is Callicles forced to admit that this is the happy life?
How does Socrates respond? In his view, what absurd consequences seem to follow?
To what familiar distinction does Socrates leads us in light of these consequences? (Familiar in the sense that Aristotle made a similar kind of distinction.)
Why won’t Callicles admit that pleasure and the good are not identical?
Why is this a problem for Socrates?
Following Callicles’ refusal are two separate arguments that try to establish that the good and the pleasant are different. Can you discern the “essential” premises of these two arguments?
The leaky jars example is introduced to illustrate the two rival kinds of lives being proposed by Callicles and Socrates.
Which life seems like the best life to you?
Why does it seem like the best life?
What concerns do you have about the alternative?
