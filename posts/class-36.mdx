<QuestionBlock type="reading-content">
<Question level={1}>How does Socrates turn the discussion in a new direction at the opening of this section?</Question>
</QuestionBlock>
<QuestionBlock type="reading-content">
<Question level={1}>Why does Callicles believe self-discipline to be “folly”?</Question>
</QuestionBlock>
<QuestionBlock type="reading-content">
<Question level={1}>Accordingly, to Callicles, why do we typically praise self-control/temperance and justice?</Question>
<Question level={2}>Does he take a different view towards courage?</Question>
</QuestionBlock>
<QuestionBlock type="reading-content">
<Question level={1}>In short, what is the happy life according to Callicles (bttm. of p. 79)?</Question>
<Question level={2}>And why aren’t most people capable of living it? What do they lack?</Question>
</QuestionBlock>
<QuestionBlock type="reading-content">
<Question level={1}>In response, Socrates invokes a couple of examples involving leaky jars. What is his point here? What do these examples illustrate?</Question>
</QuestionBlock>
<QuestionBlock type="reading-content">
<Question level={1}>On page 81, what is the alternative vision of the happy life that Socrates wants to present to Callicles?</Question>
</QuestionBlock>
<QuestionBlock type="reading-content">
<Question level={1}>Socrates proposes another example of Callicles’ description of the happy life: the life of perpetual scratching and itching (pp. 82-83).</Question>
<Question level={2}>Why is Callicles forced to admit that this is the happy life?</Question>
<Question level={2}>How does Socrates respond? In his view, what absurd consequences seem to follow?</Question>
<Question level={2}>To what familiar distinction does Socrates leads us in light of these consequences? (Familiar in the sense that Aristotle made a similar kind of distinction.)</Question>
</QuestionBlock>
<QuestionBlock type="reading-content">
<Question level={1}>Why won’t Callicles admit that pleasure and the good are not identical?</Question>
<Question level={2}>Why is this a problem for Socrates?</Question>
</QuestionBlock>
<QuestionBlock type="reading-content">
<Question level={1}>Following Callicles’ refusal are two separate arguments that try to establish that the good and the pleasant are different. Can you discern the “essential” premises of these two arguments?</Question>
</QuestionBlock>
<QuestionBlock type="reading-discussion">
<Question level={1}>The leaky jars example is introduced to illustrate the two rival kinds of lives being proposed by Callicles and Socrates.</Question>
<Question level={2}>Which life seems like the best life to you?</Question>
<Question level={2}>Why does it seem like the best life?</Question>
<Question level={2}>What concerns do you have about the alternative?</Question>
