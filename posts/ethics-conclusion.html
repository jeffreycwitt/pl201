Aristotle, Ethics, Book X, cc. 6-8

Questions for: Aristotle, Ethics, Book X, cc. 6-8

Note: We will be spending most of our class on Monday with the earlier reading on Friendship.

Book X.6-8 closes the Ethics in the way Book I opened, with a discussion of the good life.
Is book X's conclusions about the good life consistent with Book I (and the things we've learned along the way)?
Just for the sake of review, note that in chapter 6, he states "happiness is not a state/disposition".
Can you understand why he makes this distinction? Is this consistent with what we've read so far? (Another way of asking the question would be: why are happiness and virtue not identical? What is different about them?)
What's the difference between a virtuous activity and a "pleasant amusement" (c. 6)?
What does Aristotle recognize as the "highest activity" (c. 7)?
Are there any reasons given to support this conclusion? (There are at least six.)
How does this life differ from the life lived according to "practical virtues" (c. 7, near 1177a22)?
Why does he say that the contemplative life is "too high for man"?
Is it odd that he would still urge us to pursue this life?
A few more differences between the "two lives" are discussed in c. 8.
How do these two lives differ in terms of needing external goods?
In chapter 7, why does Aristotle state that the life of contemplation is the most self-sufficient life (see in particular pp. 440-441)?
Why does he say it is more self-sufficient than the life of the politician or the warrior?
Can you think of an example to illustrate the difference he is trying to describe?
