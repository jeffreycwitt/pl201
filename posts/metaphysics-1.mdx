# Class Notes: Metaphysics, Book 1: A History of "Explanation"

## Wisdom as the pursuit of explanation (Book 1, chapter 1)

First let's find our way into what Aristotle is up to in the Metaphysics.

Question: In the first chapter -- what appears to be his goal or the stated subject matter of the Metaphysics.

 > 981a25 (p. 222) "nonetheless, we attribute knowing and comprehending to craft more than to experience, and we judge the craftsmen are wiser than experienced people...This is because the craftsmen knows the cause, but merely experienced people do not..."

Question: Why? Why do we think the craftsman to be wiser than a person who merely has some experience? 

Consider for example that the "person of experience" is me, in my house, knowing that if I turn on this faucet, water will come on. 

How am I different from the plumber who has never been to my house? In this case, when walking into the house, who would be able to turn on the water faster? 

Probably, me, the person with experience in this particular house. So in this particular case it is the person of "experience" that is able to solve the particular problem faster.

But why then does Aristotle say that the craftsman is wiser than the person of experience?

Well, we might also consider the limits of the "person of experience".

If the plumber and I were both dropped into some kind of modern fancy house with unique and strange faucets that neither of us have ever seen before, who would best be able to figure out how to turn on the water faster?

Probably the plumber because he understands the "causes" of how faucets work in general. Using this knowledge he can trace where the water enters the house, which pipes it flows through, and eventually follow it to the faucet. 

The person of experience, who doesn't know anything about plumbing in general, would not be able to do this. The person of experience only knows that if you flip switch X in their own particular house, then the water comes on. But their mere knowledge of this "fact" doesn't help them in new and unfamiliar situations.

So, as Aristotle says: 

> 982a4 (p. 224), "it is clear, then, that wisdom is knowledge of certain sorts of principles and causes"

It is the plumber's **knowledge of the causes** of how water flows through pipes and faucets that sets them apart.

And since Aristotle's goal in this book is to study and identify the highest wisdom, this observation leads him to the conclusion that the science of the highest wisdom should be concerned with the most fundamental causes and principles.

He writes again:

> c. 2, 982a5 (p. 224): "Since this is the science we are looking for, we should consider what sorts of causes and principles wisdom is the science of."

This observation leads him to the conclusion that, before we do this, we must catalogue and identify what a cause is and how many different kinds of causes there are.

## A history of explanation 

At this point in our course, we've reached the culmination of our historical study of cause and explanation. We've seen many different theories, each with their own promise and problems.

In each of these theories, Aristotle sees progress toward a complete comprehension of causality/explanation in all its dimensions.

Accordingly, the opening book of the Metaphysics provides us with a kind of mini-history of philosophy, in which he walks through the discovery of different kinds of causes and explains how each one by itself is incomplete and then leads to the next. 

So, let's walk with him through the history and see the complete picture that he arrives at.

### The Material Cause.

The first cause Aristotle singles out is the one he thinks is most obvious, which is the "matter and subject" (983a25-35, p. 227). Today we traditionally call this the "material cause".

Aristotle notes that it was this "material cause" that fascinated and pre-occupied the earliest Pre-Socratic thinkers: 

He writes:

> 983b7, p. 227: "Most of the first philosophers, then, thought that the only principles of all things were material."

But he also notes what we observed in class during our reading of the Pre-Socratics, that this focus on the material cause led to a very particular conclusion about change.

Namely, that change is impossible.

> 983b12, (p. 228 top): "And for this reason they think that nothing either comes to be or is destroyed on the assumption that this..."

To avoid this problem, Aristotle notes that philosophers began to push -- first in subtle and exploratory ways -- beyond the material cause toward other kinds of explanations.

### The Efficient Cause

For example, earlier thinkers began to recognize that if change can be accounted for by the different arrangements of matter, then something must account for what pushes and pulls the matter into different arrangements.

We can see this when Aristotle writes: 

> (984a19, p. 229): "For however true it might be that all coming to be and perishing is from one (or more than one) thing, still, why does this happen, and what is the cause? For certainly the subject does not produce change in itself. I mean, for instance, neither wood nor the bronze causes itself to change would itself produce a bed, or the bronze a statue, but something else causes the change. And the search for this is (in or view) to search for a second principle -- the source and principle of motion."

Aristotle thinks we can see this in a theory like the one proposed by Empedocles (who was a teacher of Anaxagoras) who posited "love" and "strife," which explain why matter moves and takes shape in a particular way. (This is not too dissimilar from the constant movement (flux) identified by Heraclitus or the role of Nous (Mind) in Anaxagoras's theory to explain how matter gets moved and separated into different arrangements.)

In short, if we explain change as the movement and re-arrangement of matter, what explains or causes this matter to move and re-arrange?

Traditionally today, we call this the "efficient cause".

### The Formal Cause

At the bottom of p. 230, we get the suggestion of a new advance. At this point, we need to ask, what part of reality is still left unexplained by these first two causes?

Aristotle writes: 

> p. 230, "After these sorts of principles were proposed by these people, other people found them inadequate to generate the **nature of beings**; once again, as we said, it was as though the truth itself compelled them, and so they began to search for the next sort of principle. For presumably it is unlikely that fire or earth or anything else of that sort would cause some things to be in a good and fine state and would cause other things to come to be in that state."

In other words, the material and efficient cause alone don't seem to explain why the formation that results from arranging and re-arranging matter is a certain **type of thing**, or why we call it this type of thing, nor why it generates or causes other things like itself. 

More concretely, matter and a thing that pushes around matter doesn't explain why a change results in a tree or why it has all the particular features of a tree (the structural capacities and abilities that we expect from a tree). Nor does it explain why another movement of the same matter can be called a dog with all of the capabilities and potentialities that we associate with a dog. We only start calling something a tree when the matter becomes arranged and functions in a very precise way.

Or using our Eiffel Tower example: it seems highly unlikely that the random influx of energy would re-arrange steel in the form of the Eiffel Tower. It seems much more likely that the builders began with **the idea of the tower** in their minds and then began arranging the matter accordingly.

Notice that Aristotle says it is:

> 984b14 (p. 230) "unsatisfactory to entrust so great a result to chance and luck" 

It's just not causally sufficient to say that it is an accident that the matter arranged in such and such a way always produces or gives birth to the same kind of thing.

So, this "idea of a certain type of thing" which explains why we perceive a world that is not just composed of elemental particles but of distinct species and substances must be another type of cause or explanation. 

This was the level of explanation that Plato was most concerned with, and we see him trying to address this with his "Theory of Forms".

Accordingly, Aristotle calls this the "Formal Cause".

### Critique of Plato's theory of the forms

At this point, however, we need to pause and recognize that while Aristotle is completely committed to the idea of formal causal, he is also critical of Plato's specific account of how this works.

> (c. 6, 987a4, p. 233) "Plato agreed with Socrates, but because of his Heraclitean views he took these definitions to apply not to perceptible things but to other things; for, he thought, the common formula could not be of any of the perceptible things, since they are always changing. Beings of this sort [that definitions are of], then, he called Ideas, and he said that perceptible things are apart from these, and are called them, since the things with the same names are the Forms are what they are by participation in them."

Let's just note two of his many criticisms, both of which are included in the previously distributed "Criticisms of Plato's Forms" handout. 

1) First, he is critical of the notion of "participation." Plato doesn't explain how these separate and independent ideas interact with the world of matter.

> 987b11, p. 233, "In speaking of participation he changed only the name…but they left it to others to investigate what it is to participate in or to imitate Forms"

2) Second, he suggests that in seeking to explain this world -- the one you and I inhabit -- Plato found it necessary to invent another world.

> 990a43, p. 235, c. 9: "The first objection is that in seeking to grasp the causes of beings in this world, they introduced different things, equal in number to them. It is as though someone wanted to count things and thought he could not do it if there were fewer of them, but could do it if he added more."

That is as close as you can get to a philosophy slam.

### Aristotle's view of the forms

To see the substance of Aristotle's critique more clearly, let's consider Aristotle's alternative view of "forms".

First let's consider this very famous painting of Plato and Aristotle by Raphael, the "School of Athens".
![School of Athens](https://s3.amazonaws.com/lum-faculty-jcwitt-public/pl201/schoolOfAthens.png)

Question: Can you tell who is Plato and who is Aristotle in this painting?

The difference is that Plato is pointing up to the heavens, while Aristotle is pointing down to the earth. And Raphael painted them this way to capture the fundamental difference between their views on the forms.

For Plato, the forms are fundamentally separated from the physical world. They exist in a separate realm, and the physical world is just an imitation or approximation of the world of forms. This is why Plato is pointing up. He is pointing up to the world of the forms. 

For Aristotle, the forms are not separate from the physical world. While they are still immaterial, they are nevertheless part of the physical world.  This is why Aristotle is pointing down. He is pointing down to this world where the forms are found.

We can see this difference more clearly in the diagram below.

![Plato vs. Aristotle on Forms](https://s3.amazonaws.com/lum-faculty-jcwitt-public/pl201/PlatoVsAristotleOnForms.png)

Aristotle's alternative approach is to think of the forms as tied to individual physical things. This means that each individual dog contains or possesses its own form of "dog-ness." But even though each "dog" possesses its own form, this form of "dog-ness" is identical with the form of dog-ness in any and every other dog.

Plato might have an objection here. Plato might say: we now have a plurality of dog-ness -- therefore not one idea of dog-ness but lots of ideas of dog-ness -- how do we account for the fact that we think there is only one IDEA of dog-ness.

Aristotle's response invokes the ACTIVE/Constructive POWER OF THE KNOWING MIND. 

It is true Aristotle says that "in number" there is a form of dog-ness for each dog. 

But in content, the idea of dog-ness is one because each form of dog-ness in each dog has identical content. Thus, the mind can look at any dog and "abstract" the idea of dog-ness from an individual dog and can rest assured that it has understood the form of all the other dogs as well.

Here there is no need to posit "Recollection" or an encounter with "Dog-ness" in a previous life. 

Our active mind can encounter dog-ness1, 2, or 3, or in any of its material instantiations and then abstract out the general concept from its particular instantiation through "induction".

At this point, we might have one lingering question for Aristotle:

If the form in all the individuals is identical in type, what makes each of these individuals different? If the form dog-ness is exactly the same in each dog, what makes all of these dogs different individual dogs?

The only answer he can give is the matter. Thus for Aristotle matter is the "principle of individuation".

![Aristotle on Individuation](https://s3.amazonaws.com/lum-faculty-jcwitt-public/pl201/AristotleOnIndividuation.png)

But this does raise some interesting new questions. What if my matter completely changes? Am I a new individual, or am I still somehow the same person?


### The Final Cause

Finally, Aristotle still thinks there is one missing piece; one further explanation that explains why a form carries within it the kinds of capacities and potencies that it has and why it demands a certain kind of matter to be what it is.

Example: 

Let's consider the example of a hammer.

We know that it is the form or idea of a hammer that dictates that it must be made of a certain kind of material because, to be a hammer, it must have certain kinds of potentiality and capabilities. Likewise, the idea, design, or blueprint of a hammer is what tells us that it must have this particular shape and structure in order to be a hammer.

But what determined this "design" in the first place? How do we know what kinds of powers and capabilities a hammer needed to have to be a hammer? Why did it originally get designed this way?

Aristotle pushes the set of questions back one step further and asks what motivated the designer of the hammer to design it this way? What motivated the designer to invent a hammer -- with these particular capabilities and powers -- in the first place?

Before the idea of a hammer was generated for the first time, there was a need for a tool that could drive nails into wood. There was an original goal or desire or purpose. It was this goal or purpose that **caused** the inventor of the hammer to come up with the idea in the first place. This need or goal, then, is also a cause or explanation of the hammer.

Aristotle calls this the Final cause -- final because it is the end or goal that motivates the design and production of the hammer. But it is also in a way the "first" cause because it is the cause that "gets the ball rolling." It is the cause that determines the idea or form of the hammer, which in turn determines what kind of material and efficient cause are needed to actually produce the hammer.

This is a cause that Aristotle thinks everyone before him has overlooked, but is essential if we are going to give a complete account of the world.
