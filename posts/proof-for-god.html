Questions for Aristotle Metaphysics, Book XII

What is the central puzzle introduced in Book XII, c. 6?
Why does Aristotle say that there "must be a principle of the sort whose essence is actuality"?
What problem would occur if everything in the world only had the potentiality for motion or change, but there was no actuality that could come along and initiate that change?
How is Plato said to address this issue?
In the first paragraph of chapter 7, what are the two kinds of movers that Aristotle introduces to solve the problem introduced in chapter 6?
How are these movers similar?
How are these movers different?
Consider how this analysis of eternal motion in terms of an unmoved mover and everlasting circular movement is mapped on to Aristotle's vision of the stars and the planets in chapter 8.
Finally, in chapter 9, how does Aristotle describe the divine life of the unmoved mover?
Why does he argue that, if the unmoved mover is the best substance, it will be engaged in an ever-lasting contemplation of itself?

Questions for Copleston and Russell, 'A Debate on the Argument from Contingency'

First a note: Before getting bogged down in the details of this debate, try to step back and appreciate the whole. Whether or not you can follow every argument or claim that is made, observe in general how important the notion of "cause" or "explanation" is in this debate. Here we can we see how critical these metaphysical concepts are to foundational questions like, 'Does God exists?'. A key goal here is just to see how debates in metaphysics are conducted in the modern area and to appreciate how the questions that occupied the ancient philosophers continue to be relevant today.

What definition of God does Copleston begin with?

What is a "necessary being"?
Why does Copleston think a universe of "contingent beings" demands a "necessary being"?

After considerable back and forth on pages 6-8, at the very bottom of page 8b, Russell points out that the real crux of the matter is that Copleston believes everything must have a "sufficient reason".

Can you get a sense of why Copleston believes there must be a "sufficient reason" for everything?
What happens to our understanding of the universe if there is a sufficient reason for everything? (See page 9b.)
Why doesn't this bother Russell?
