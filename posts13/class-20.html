Class 20 Guided Reading Questions

Plato, Phaedo, 107-133 (70a-95a)

show participation instructions

What new question does Cebes introduce at 70a?
How does Socrates attempt to demonstrate that the soul is immortal? (See 70e to 72a)
How does his theory about recollection from the Meno reappear here? (See 73a and following.)
How is Socrates using the "theory of the forms" and his "theory of recollection" to prove that the soul is eternal? (Consider 75d-77d.)
Consider Socrates' explanation of why the soul is not scattered at death (78a-79a).
What characteristics does the soul share with the "forms"?
What is Socrates' explanation of how the soul becomes re-incarnated into a material being (81a-84c)?
Why are some souls "heavy"?
Why are these "heavy" souls dragged back to earth?
How does philosophy make the soul "light" and free, able to transcend the material world and remain in the world of the forms/ideas?
What objections do Simmias and Cebes introduce (86a-88c, see also 91d, for a restatement of these positions)?
What does Socrates have to say about misology (hatred of reason)? How does it arise in the same way that misology (hatred of people) arises (89d ff.)?
In what way might Socrates have the Sophists and/or Pre-Socratics in the back of his mind here?
How does Socrates respond to Simmias' argument?
How does he use the argument from recollection to defeat this argument?
Why does he think the hypothesis that the soul is a harmony leads to the absurd conclusion that everyone is equally good (94a)?
Within Socrates arguments about the eternality of the soul, what sort of ethical implications do you see emerging?
What, for examples, does his account of the different ways that the soul can be incarnated (81a-84c) say about how life should be lived? what the goal of life is? or why philosophy is important?
