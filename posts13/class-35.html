Class 35 Guided Reading Questions

Plato's Gorgias, 481b-492c, pp. 62-78

show participation instructions

Where does Callicles think Polus has gone wrong?
With Polus especially, Socrates tried to point out the excessive reliance on appearance and neglect of the unchanging truth. Here, how does Callicles invert this charge and accuse Socrates of paying attention to appearances only (p. 65)?
What kind of distinction does Callicles appeal to in order to make his point?
According to Callicles, who are the people that make the laws? What kind of people are they?
What kind of laws do they make? Why?
What is Callicles' complaint against philosophy (p. 67)?
Why does he say philosophers are “completely out of touch with human nature”?
What does Callicles think is the appropriate reason to pursue philosophy?
Why does Socrates praise Callicles' speech?
How is he said to differ from Gorgias and Polus (p. 70-71)?
What are the complications Socrates raises for Callicles' position?
What distinctions get drawn between 'superior', 'stronger', 'better', 'cleverest'?
What is Socrates' point by bringing up the examples of the doctor, coat maker, cobbler? (p. 76-77)
How might it problematize the belief that the 'cleverest' should have the most?
Finally, what is Callicles' final definition of what it means to be 'superior'?
Does it share any similarities with Polus' earliest description of the rhetorician? How so?
How does Callicles's evaluation of philosophy parallel some of the ways we saw—at the beginning of the semester—people valuing the utility of a “liberal arts education” without necessarily valuing the "liberal arts" (see pp. 67-68)?
